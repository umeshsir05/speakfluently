Auxiliary Verbs (सहायक क्रियाएँ)

Auxiliary verbs (also called helping verbs) are verbs that help the main verb to form tenses, moods, voices, or questions. They cannot usually stand alone as the main verb in a sentence.

Examples:
- She is writing a letter. (is – auxiliary, writing – main verb)
- They have finished their work. (have – auxiliary, finished – main verb)
- Do you like coffee? (do – auxiliary, like – main verb)

Types of Auxiliary Verbs

There are two main types: Primary Auxiliaries and Modal Auxiliaries.

1. Primary Auxiliaries (प्राथमिक सहायक क्रियाएँ)

These are: Be, Do, Have. They can also function as main verbs.

AuxiliaryFormsUsesExamples
Beam, is, are, was, were, being, beenContinuous tenses, passive voice, to be + adjective/nounHe is sleeping. / The cake was eaten.
Dodo, does, didQuestions, negatives, emphasisDo you know? / I do not like it. / She does work hard.
Havehave, has, had, havingPerfect tensesThey have arrived. / She had finished.

📌 Primary Auxiliary 'Be' – Detailed Uses

  • Continuous tenses: I am going. / They were playing.
  • Passive voice: The letter was written.
  • To be + complement: She is a doctor. / We are happy.
  • To be + infinitive (plan/arrangement): We are to meet at 5 PM.

📌 Primary Auxiliary 'Do' – Detailed Uses

  • Questions: Do you speak English? / Did she come?
  • Negatives: I do not know. / He doesn't like tea.
  • Emphasis (affirmative): I do want to help! / She does work hard.
  • To avoid repetition (short answers): "Do you like it?" "Yes, I do."

📌 Primary Auxiliary 'Have' – Detailed Uses

  • Present Perfect: I have seen that movie.
  • Past Perfect: She had already left when I arrived.
  • Future Perfect: They will have finished by noon.
  • Have to (obligation): We have to go now.

2. Modal Auxiliaries (मोडल सहायक क्रियाएँ)

Modals express possibility, necessity, permission, ability, obligation, etc. They are followed by the base form of the main verb (without 'to').

ModalMeaning/UseExample
CanAbility, permission, possibilityI can swim. / You can go now. / It can rain today.
CouldPast ability, polite request, possibilityShe could run fast. / Could you help me? / It could be true.
MayPermission, possibility (formal)You may leave. / It may rain.
MightLess certain possibilityHe might come late.
WillFuture, intention, promiseI will call you. / It will rain.
WouldPast habit, polite request, conditionalHe would visit us. / Would you like tea? / If I were you, I would go.
ShallFuture (I/we), suggestion, obligationI shall return. / Shall we dance? / You shall obey.
ShouldAdvice, obligation, expectationYou should study. / The train should arrive soon.
MustStrong obligation, necessity, certaintyYou must stop. / It must be true.
Ought toMoral obligation, adviceYou ought to respect elders.
Used toPast habit, past stateI used to smoke. / There used to be a park.
NeedNecessity (negative/interrogative)You need not worry. / Need I go?
DareCourage to do something (negative/interrogative)He dare not speak. / Dare you challenge me?

Important Rules for Using Modals

  • Modals are always followed by the base form of the main verb (no -s, -ed, -ing).
    ✓ She can sing. ✗ She can sings.
  • Modals do not change form for person or number (no 's' for third person).
    ✓ He may come. ✗ He may comes.
  • To form negatives, add 'not' after the modal.
    cannot (can't), will not (won't), must not (mustn't).
  • To form questions, invert the subject and modal.
    Can you help me? / Should we go?
  • Modals have no past tense forms (except could, would, should, might). Use other expressions to talk about past ability/obligation (e.g., "had to" for past obligation).

Differences Between Similar Modals

PairDifferenceExamples
Can vs. MayCan = ability/informal permission; May = formal permission/possibilityCan you swim? / May I come in?
Will vs. ShallWill = future for all; Shall = formal future with I/we, suggestionsI will go. / Shall we start?
Should vs. MustShould = advice (less strong); Must = strong obligation/necessityYou should exercise. / You must stop smoking.
Must vs. Have toMust = internal obligation (speaker's feeling); Have to = external obligation (rules)I must study (I feel). / I have to work (my job requires).
Could vs. Was able toCould = general ability; Was able to = specific achievementI could swim (general). / I was able to escape (specific).

Modal Verbs in Different Tenses

Present/Future: can, may, will, shall, must, should, etc. + base verb
He can speak French. / You must go now.

Past forms: could, would, should, might + base verb (for hypothetical or reported past)
She said she would come. / I could run fast when I was young.

Perfect modals (past possibility/regret): modal + have + past participle (V3)
You should have called me. / He might have forgotten. / She must have left.

Practice Exercises

Fill in the blanks with the correct auxiliary verb (primary or modal):

1. She ______ (be) working on the project right now.
2. ______ you like some coffee? (polite request)
3. I ______ (have) already finished my homework.
4. You ______ (must/should) not smoke here; it's prohibited.
5. When I was a child, I ______ (can/could) climb trees easily.
6. ______ (Do/Does) he speak English?
7. We ______ (shall/will) meet at the station tomorrow.
8. She ______ (might/must) be at home; the lights are on.
9. You ______ (ought to / need to) apologize for your mistake.
10. He ______ (dare/dared) not tell the truth.
Answers:
1. is 2. Would 3. have 4. must 5. could 6. Does 7. will 8. must 9. ought to 10. dare
Remember: Primary auxiliaries (be, do, have) help form tenses and questions. Modal auxiliaries express mood (possibility, necessity, permission). Learn their specific uses to improve your English accuracy.
