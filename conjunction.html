Conjunction (संयोजक)

A conjunction is a word that joins two words, phrases, clauses, or sentences together. It connects elements of equal grammatical rank or shows relationship between clauses.

Examples:
- Ram and Shyam are friends. (joins two nouns)
- She is poor but honest. (joins two adjectives)
- I stayed home because it was raining. (joins two clauses)

Types of Conjunctions

1. Coordinating Conjunctions (समानाधिकरण संयोजक)

These join two independent clauses or elements of equal grammatical rank. There are seven: For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So (FANBOYS).

ConjunctionFunctionExample
Forreason/purposeHe is happy, for he won the prize.
AndadditionI bought apples and oranges.
Nornegative additionHe doesn't eat meat, nor does he eat fish.
ButcontrastShe is young but wise.
Oralternative/choiceWould you like tea or coffee?
YetcontrastHe worked hard, yet he failed.
Soresult/consequenceIt was late, so we went home.

2. Subordinating Conjunctions (अधीनस्थ संयोजक)

These join a dependent clause to an independent clause, showing a relationship such as time, cause, condition, contrast, or purpose.

Common Subordinating Conjunctions: after, although, as, because, before, if, since, than, though, unless, until, when, where, while, as soon as, as long as, in order that, provided that.

Examples:
- I will stay until you return. (time)
- He passed because he studied hard. (reason)
- If it rains, we will cancel the picnic. (condition)
- Although he is rich, he is humble. (contrast)

3. Correlative Conjunctions (युग्म संयोजक)

These are pairs of conjunctions that work together to join equal elements.

PairExample
either...orYou can have either tea or coffee.
neither...norHe is neither tall nor short.
both...andShe is both intelligent and hardworking.
not only...but alsoHe not only plays cricket but also sings.
whether...orI don't know whether he will come or not.
so...as (negative)He is not so clever as his brother.
as...as (positive)She is as beautiful as her mother.

Important Rules for Conjunctions

✅ Subject-Verb Agreement with Conjunctions

  • When two subjects are joined by and, the verb is plural.
    Ram and Shyam are playing.
  • With either...or / neither...nor, the verb agrees with the nearest subject.
    Either Sita or her sisters have come.
    Neither the boys nor the teacher is present.
  • With not only...but also, the verb agrees with the second subject.
    Not only the students but also the teacher was absent.

✅ Punctuation Rules

  • When a coordinating conjunction joins two independent clauses, use a comma before the conjunction.
    I wanted to go, but I was too tired.
  • When the independent clauses are short, the comma is optional.
    She laughed and danced.
  • When a subordinating conjunction begins a sentence, use a comma after the dependent clause.
    Although it rained, we went out.

❌ Common Mistakes

  • Don't use and and but together in the same clause.
  • Don't use because and so together (redundant).
    Because he was ill, so he didn't come. (✗)
    Because he was ill, he didn't come. (✓)
  • Don't use although and but together.
    Although he is rich, but he is humble. (✗)
    Although he is rich, he is humble. (✓)

List of Common Conjunctions

Coordinating: for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so (FANBOYS)
Subordinating: after, although, as, because, before, if, since, than, though, unless, until, when, where, while, as soon as, as long as, in order that, provided that, so that
Correlative: either...or, neither...nor, both...and, not only...but also, whether...or, as...as, so...as

Practice Exercises

Fill in the blanks with the correct conjunction (answers below):
1. She is smart ______ hardworking. (and/but)
2. He worked hard, ______ he failed. (so/yet)
3. You will succeed ______ you work hard. (if/although)
4. ______ he is old, he is active. (Because/Although)
5. He is ______ a singer ______ a dancer. (both...and / either...or)
6. ______ Ram ______ Shyam has come. (Neither...nor / Both...and)
7. Wait here ______ I return. (until/because)
8. She studied well ______ she passed the exam. (so/because)
Answers:
1. and 2. yet 3. if 4. Although 5. both...and 6. Neither...nor 7. until 8. so
Remember: Conjunctions are the glue that holds sentences together. Coordinating conjunctions join equal parts; subordinating conjunctions show a relationship of dependence; correlative conjunctions work in pairs. Practice identifying them in everyday reading.
